Trends across 2024–26 winners

T1

Satirical product-page as portfolio flex

Studios ship fake products (Oryzo AI cork coaster) with full hero / spec / testimonial / academic-paper stack. The “product” is the joke; the craft is the point. Fully replaces the traditional case-study index.

T2

Gated narrative: hold-and-drag to unlock

The hero demands a physical gesture (hold, drag, long-press) before the site proceeds. Siena Film Foundation’s scroll-unlock is the cleanest example. Rewards attention, filters low-intent visitors.

T3

Scroll-scrubbed timelines over static case studies

Getty TracingArt scrolls a painting’s ownership across centuries. The timeline replaces the gallery. Works for provenance, careers, product changelogs — anywhere time is the spine.

T4

Forward-slash and bracket nav as UI identity

The Line uses / About, Inkfish uses [01]–[17] and [S.01]. Tiny typographic tokens do the work a logo used to do: every label reads as branded without drawing a logo.

T5

Manifesto H1 replaces product H1 on AI sites

Zentry: “REDEFINE / Enter the Human-Agentic OS.” Oryzo: “Made for mugs. Built for tables.” 2024’s AI landings explained features; 2026’s declare a worldview. Worth stealing for dr-cv’s “Agentic Designer” tagline.

T6

Counter-cycling numerals (0–9 tickers) as texture

Terminal Industries cycles 01→09 tickers next to benefit blocks, feels industrial-dashboard. Cheap to implement, high perceived fidelity. Replaces stat-counter animation.

T7

“Scroll to discover” as the only CTA above the fold

Montfort, Siena, David Whyte all push the primary action to a scroll cue, not a button. The hero becomes a poster; the product lives below. Inverts 2022–23 SaaS landing orthodoxy.

T8

Editorial serif for the argument, sans for the chrome

David Whyte, Siena, Getty all pair a serif for the poetic / quoted / story content with a neutral sans for nav and metadata. The inverse of 2021 (sans-everywhere). Serif signals trust and patience.

T9

Numbered portfolio with duration chips

Inkfish numbers every project [01]–[17] and shows runtime on hover (1:26). Quantifies the archive, makes a 17-item list feel curated instead of overwhelming.

T10

Proof-of-work via the doc site itself

Anime.js proves the library works by animating its own docs. The demo isn’t in a sandbox, it’s the page. For dr-cv: the site itself should be the clearest demonstration of “built with agents.”

Oryzo AI — AI-powered cork coaster

oryzo.ai
SOTM Apr 2026 satirical product Lusion studio
ORYZO AI
Made for mugs. Built for tables.

Hero / opening act

A dead-serious product page for a cork coaster that is pretending to be an AI-breakthrough device. The hero frames it as a “portable, stable, circular” piece of hardware complete with academic paper, testimonials, and spec tables. The joke lands because the craft never breaks.

The “Encode/Decode Message” smart-flip encryption section — flipping the coaster on-screen switches content states, making the physical metaphor interactive.

Copy + narrative

14 sections before the footer. Tone: manifesto-as-satire — mimics AI hype so faithfully it doubles as critique.

Gap analysis for dr-cv — what R5 fills

Rounds 1–4 gave dr-cv the AI-landing grammar, the personal-portfolio archetypes, and the studio theatrics. What they missed was the editorial half of 2024–26: sites that win without WebGL, that trust typography, and that use gesture / gating / scroll-scrubbing to create weight. R5 plugs those gaps.

1. Satirical product-as-portfolio replaces the case-study grid

Neither R3 portfolios nor R4 studios ship “fake products.” Oryzo AI shows you can package yourself as a product launch complete with spec tables and testimonials. Fits dr-cv’s “Agentic Designer” positioning better than yet another project grid.

apply to → hero + work section fusion

2. Scroll-scrubbed timeline replaces the chronological CV block

Getty TracingArt scrubs 275 years of a painting’s ownership on a single scroll. Applied to dr-cv: one scroll scrubs 2018→2026 across career checkpoints, with artifacts / clients / outcomes revealed per step. No R1–R4 site models this; we previously defaulted to year-tagged cards.

apply to → career timeline / “narrative gap” repair

3. Gated entry (hold-and-drag) filters drive-by traffic into the case studies

Siena Film’s scroll-unlock — and Awwwards rewarding it — shows that friction can be a signal of quality. Applies to the BAH / Trusted Environments case study specifically: a gesture gate before the anonymised artifacts. Reads as “this wasn’t for everybody” without needing an NDA banner.

apply to → confidential case-study entry

4. Bracket / slash nav gives dr-cv a typographic logo for free

The Line’s / About and Inkfish’s [01]–[17] do what R3’s dev portfolios achieved through monospace density, but with one character. For a solo site without a wordmark budget, this is the highest leverage identity move R5 surfaces.

apply to → section headings + work numbering

5. One-sentence poster hero (Montfort / Zentry / Oryzo)

R2’s AI landings always pair H1 with a subhead and CTA cluster. R5 shows 2025–26 winners stripping the hero down to a single 120px sentence and a scroll cue. Gives dr-cv’s “Agentic Designer” tagline more weight than any animated subhead could.

apply to → hero composition

6. Editorial serif + sans pairing for the “serio + cálido” brand

R3 portfolios were almost all sans-only or mono-only. R5 (David Whyte, Siena, Getty) proves serif carries patience and trust better than sans — which is exactly the tone brief for dr-cv. Serif for quotes and section openers, sans for chrome, mono for dates / versions. Three-family discipline, not monoculture.

apply to → typography system
