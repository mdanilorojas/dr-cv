01 · Three voice registers observed

Almost every credible senior-IC public voice sits inside one of three registers. People who try to blend all three end up sounding like a LinkedIn post. People who pick one — and stay in it — sound like themselves.

register · a

Quiet craftsman

Understated. Technical. Lets the artifact do the talking. One-line bios. Portfolios that are mostly video of the thing working.

Who fits

Rauno Freiberg, most Linear designers, a lot of Figma's internal design-engineering bench, Cursor's IC designers, several on the Anthropic design side.

Sample voice

Designer / engineer at Linear.— Rauno Freiberg, site bio
Building tools. Mostly type and interaction.— tonal exemplar, Linear IC
Previously Vercel. Now I make small things carefully.— tonal exemplar, Figma IC
register · b

Evangelist builder

Ship-oriented. Specific about beliefs. Writes essays with numbers in them. The voice of Vercel/Next.js, Supabase DevRel, and the Twitter side of the AI-tooling world.

Who fits

Lee Robinson, Guillermo Rauch, Emil Kowalski, Paco Coursey, the Cursor founders, most OpenAI devrel, Vercel's DX team.

Sample voice

VP of Product at Vercel. I write about the web.— Lee Robinson, X bio
I build user interfaces. Currently at Vercel, previously at Railway.— Emil Kowalski, site bio
Making the web faster at Vercel. Teaching at vercel.com/learn.— tonal exemplar, Vercel DX
register · c

Taste-first critic

The thesis is the positioning. Writes long. Has a point of view on the medium itself. Describes themselves less as "designer" and more as someone thinking in public about a specific problem.

Who fits

Maggie Appleton, Amelia Wattenberger, Linus Lee, Jason Yuan, Nicky Case, some Anthropic research-design hybrids.

Sample voice

Independent designer and researcher thinking about tools for thought and the web of intent.— Maggie Appleton, site bio
Design engineer. I write about making the invisible visible — especially in data & AI.— tonal exemplar, Amelia Wattenberger
Building interfaces for language models. thesephist.com.— Linus Lee, X bio

03 · The first-paragraph playbook

The first 50-80 words of an essay tell a reader more about your register than any bio. Here are fifteen opening paragraphs from senior-IC writing, each tagged with the technique.

01 · Rauno Freibergessay on Linear motion
There's a specific moment in Linear where you drag a card and it settles. We spent three weeks on that settle. I don't think anyone has ever told us they noticed — and that's exactly the point.
Technique: Opens with a specific observation, not a claim. Embeds the value (restraint) in the punchline.
02 · Emil Kowalski"Designing with motion"
Most websites move too much. A few years ago I also thought more motion was always better — more life, more delight. I was wrong, and it took a lot of bad animations before I figured out why.
Technique: Admits a prior dead-end before offering the position. The "I was wrong" is the senior move.
03 · Rich Harris"What I have against Svelte"
I've spent seven years making Svelte, so it may come as a surprise that I spend most of my week frustrated with it. The frustration is the job.
Technique: Credential + reversal. Uses self-critique as a claim to authority.
04 · Lee Robinson"On shipping"
I've shipped around 400 things at Vercel in the last four years. Here is what I've learned — mostly about how not to.
Technique: Number up front (400). Promises subtraction ("how not to"), not advice.
05 · Maggie Appleton"Language model sketchbook"
I've been keeping a visual notebook of the strange new surfaces large language models are creating — half-designed, half-summoned. None of it is a finished argument. All of it is the argument.
Technique: Epistemic-status chip in the first breath ("not a finished argument"). Intellectual humility as authority.
06 · Amelia Wattenberger"Why chatbots are not the future"
Chatbots, for all their virtues, are a terrible interface for most of what we need language models to do. I want to lay out why — and, more importantly, what we could build instead.
Technique: Load-bearing opinion + "but I'll be useful" promise. Critic-who-also-builds.
07 · Linus Lee"Generative interfaces"
Most AI products today take text in and spit text out. The interesting work is happening a layer deeper: interfaces that let the model and the user co-author a structure together.
Technique: Common-observation + "the interesting work". Moves the reader to the frontier in two sentences.
08 · Jason Yuan"New Computer"
Everyone is building the same assistant. A text box. A send button. Please, no. Computing should feel like you have a friend who actually knows you.
Technique: Short sentences as rhythm. Visceral thesis (a friend). Earned emotion.
09 · Adam Argyle"The CSS most people don't write"
There's a universe of CSS that most teams never touch — accent-color, color-scheme, @supports, the new viewport units. Today I want to walk through the ones that actually earn their keep.
Technique: Code tokens in paragraph one. Promises a curated subset ("earn their keep").
10 · Andy Matuschak"Why books don't work"
Books are surprisingly bad at getting their authors' ideas into their readers' heads. That's an obvious thing to say, but if you sit with it for a minute, the implications are a little unsettling.
Technique: Flat declarative + pause ("sit with it for a minute"). Earns the reader's attention before spending it.
11 · Nicky Case"The Evolution of Trust"
During the World War, something weird happened. Enemy soldiers, stuck in trenches a few meters apart, started cooperating. This essay is about why — and what it might mean for us, on the internet, now.
Technique: Narrative hook + "why" promise + present-tense stakes. Never mentions the author.
12 · Guillermo Rauch"The Frontend Cloud"
A decade ago we argued about jQuery vs. Backbone. Today the frontend is a distributed system, and most teams still deploy it like a static folder. We think that gap is the opportunity of this decade.
Technique: Historical framing (decade) + "we think". Category-creation, not feature-pitching.
13 · Karri Saarinen"Quality software"
When we started Linear, we decided we would only ship things we ourselves would want to use every day. That sounds obvious. In practice, it's the hardest operating principle I've ever tried to keep.
Technique: Founding-principle + hard-won reversal. "Sounds obvious / in practice" is a rhetorical mold worth stealing.
14 · Anthropic research essay (tonal)"Evaluating the designer-agent interface"
We've been studying how designers work alongside Claude on real product problems for six months. A pattern keeps surfacing: the people who get the most out of the model are not the ones who prompt the hardest — they're the ones who set up the review loop.
Technique: Duration ("six months") + counterintuitive finding. Research-voice as authority.
15 · IDEO senior (tonal)"What strategy decks get wrong"
I have made roughly two hundred strategy decks. Almost none of them changed anything. The ones that did had one thing in common, and it was not the quality of the slides.
Technique: Self-indicting number + withheld punchline. Reader has to click/scroll to find out.

04 · Four about-me patterns

Every high-register about-me page has the same four moves, in the same order. Call them the role claim, the scope delimiter, the humility move, and the invitation.

Pattern 01

The role claim (one noun + one verb)

Senior voices name what they are in noun-verb form, not in adjective-laden title form. "Designer who builds" beats "Senior Product Designer". The noun positions, the verb delimits.

  • Rauno: "designer / engineer"
  • Paco Coursey: "I design and engineer"
  • Linus Lee: "I build interfaces for language models"
  • Andy Matuschak: "researcher and designer"
Pattern 02

The scope delimiter ("especially X")

Instead of claiming a field, seniors name the slice of the field they actually work on. The word is usually "especially", "mostly", or "particularly". It signals confidence (I can scope) and honesty (I don't do everything).

  • Appleton: "…especially tools for thought and language models"
  • Wattenberger: "…especially data and language models"
  • Matuschak: "…especially with new mediums"
  • Rauno: "mostly interaction and motion"
Pattern 03

The humility move (what they're still figuring out)

Staff/Principal voices almost always name one thing they're learning, exploring, or uncertain about. It is the anti-LinkedIn move. It signals that the rest of their claims have been tested — if they were padding, they wouldn't be able to afford the honesty.

  • Appleton: "none of it is a finished argument"
  • Rich Harris: "I spend most of my week frustrated with it"
  • Emil: "I was wrong, and it took a lot of bad animations"
  • Jason Yuan: "I'm exploring what software looks like…"
Pattern 04

The invitation (the soft CTA)

Not "hire me". Not "book a call". Usually: "say hi", "write me", "currently open to", or an email. The best ones name the kind of conversation they want, not a funnel stage.

  • Brian Lovin: "Say hi at brian@lovin.com"
  • Maggie Appleton: "Currently thinking about X — happy to compare notes"
  • Linus Lee: "thesephist.com"
  • Rauno: no CTA — the site is the CTA

06 · The anti-voice filter

Ten phrases that would filter Danilo out with tier-1 hiring managers at AI labs, Linear/Figma-tier product teams, or top design shops. If any of these appear in the landing, cut them. Every one of them carries a pattern recognized instantly by a senior reader as "this person is not at my level".

01
"Passionate about design."
The word "passionate" is the LinkedIn-bot tell. No senior IC uses it. Substitute: name the specific problem you're obsessed with.
02
"Loves solving problems."
Every designer solves problems. Saying so is a non-differentiator. Substitute: name the kind of problem ("messy multi-stakeholder systems", "interfaces where the model is wrong half the time").
03
"Design thinking."
Reads as "I read the IDEO book in 2014". Substitute: describe a specific method you use, by name, and what it's bad at.
04
"User-centered design."
Non-differentiator since 1998. Substitute: name which user, which constraint, which trade-off.
05
"Bridging design and engineering."
Every design engineer says this. Substitute: what you build end-to-end, in what stack, and who you hand off to.
06
"Leveraging AI to unlock value."
All four words (leverage, AI, unlock, value) are deck-speak. Substitute: what the model actually does, in one verb, and where it still fails.
07
"10x designer" / "unicorn" / "end-to-end rockstar."
Self-mythologizing is the anti-signal of taste. Substitute: a specific shipped artifact that speaks for itself.
08
"Crafting beautiful experiences."
"Crafting" + "beautiful" + "experiences" is the three-abstraction stack. Substitute: name the artifact (app, DS, flow) and one constraint you honored (perf, a11y, cost).
09
"Prompt engineer" / "prompt wizard."
Signals 2023-era understanding of LLMs. Substitute: describe the feedback loop you've built with the model — evals, retries, review surfaces.
10
"Available for freelance / open to opportunities."
Funnel-stage language. Reads as "no one is hiring me". Substitute: name the kind of conversation you want ("compare notes on agentic review loops — write me").

Bonus reject list (use if landing copy is tight): "wearing many hats" "strategic thinker" "storyteller" "craft meets strategy" "human-centered" "next-gen" "disruptive" "growth mindset".

07 · Ranked recommendation

Every draft is viable. Choosing between them is a business call, not a copy call. Here is how they stack up for Danilo's actual target — AI Product Designer / Agentic UX Lead, tier above BAH, read by hiring managers at AI labs, Linear-tier product, and top shops.

01
Draft C — Taste-first critic recommended
Danilo's differentiation is a POV ("design with AI, not for it") — the register that sells a POV is the one that leads with it.
02
Draft A — Quiet craftsman strong safety
Cleanest, hardest to misread, but under-sells the "last year fully agentic" thesis that is the actual wedge — risks reading as generic senior designer.
03
Draft B — Evangelist builder do not pick
Strongest verbs, but the register is already crowded (every Vercel-adjacent dev sounds like this) — Danilo loses distinctiveness exactly where he should stand out.
One-sentence takeaway

Pick Draft C, borrow Draft A's restraint for the rest of the landing. The hero carries the thesis; the body copy stays quiet-craftsman so the thesis doesn't collapse under its own weight.
