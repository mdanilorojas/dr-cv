Signature move · one technique known for it

Warm apricot bg-clip gradient in the H1, echoed ONLY in a canvas-2D light-beam behind the live-dot.

Implementation sketch · Pure CSS for the type; 40-line canvas 2D for the glow (no three.js, no WebGL). The glow auto-disables on prefers-reduced-motion.

04The one-question pick a single framing question

one question · decides which Final ships

On first visit, do prospects need to download the artifact,
or understand the practitioner behind it?

This is not "which looks nicest." It's the strategic question that divides the three finalists cleanly by what they ask of the reader on the first 30 seconds. Every other trade-off (cost, distinctiveness, bundle, motion tier) is downstream of this answer.

If the answer is "download" → Final-2 AI Product-Tour ships the tightest funnel. The prospect lands, sees the tilted PDF stack, clicks download, reads the CV on the train. Conversion is measurable on day one. The brand feels like a SaaS. Risk: sells a tool, not a person.
If the answer is "understand the practitioner" → Final-3 Editorial Craft is the durable pick. The prospect becomes a reader; cases read as essays; trust compounds. Long funnel, high conversion on the few who finish — perfect for tier-1 consulting clients who read closely. Risk: alienates recruiters skimming in 30s.
If the answer is "both — with a bias toward understand" → Final-1 Warm Huly Refined is the honest compromise. Hero gives enough signal to download immediately; the scroll rewards readers who want the full argument; the live-data strip proves dogfooding without demanding attention. The landing carries all of Danilo's content density — 4 cases, 2 testimonials, 15-year arc, method, proof numbers — without compromising any of them. This is where R8 converged. This is where R9 ranked D1 as safest. This is where the shipping calendar is.

06Your pick + defense opinionated

round 10 verdict · no hedging

Ship Final-1 · Warm Huly Refined this week. Treat Final-3 as v2.

Final-1 is the only direction that answers every question the research surfaced with a simultaneous yes. R6 said tier LOW is enough; F1 uses CSS + canvas 2D. R7 said Warm Huly palette; F1 is Warm Huly by name. R8 said Proposal A; F1 is Proposal A verbatim. R9 said D1 was the safest with a 4/5 fit score; F1 is D1 with production hours attached. When four rounds independently converge on the same answer, the correct move is to ship what the research already proved, not to override it with taste.

The case against F1 — "too safe, reads as huly.io-adjacent" — is the weakest objection to take seriously at Phase 3, when Danilo has zero public landing live. Distinctiveness is a second-order problem; existence is first-order. An on-brief landing shipped in 4–5 days beats a distinctive landing shipped in 8 days and a visionary landing shipped in 6 weeks, especially when the cases and testimonials inside it are themselves what will distinguish Danilo — not the shader behind the hero.

Final-2's "landing is the product" framing is tempting and clever, but it subtly mis-sells. The prospect Danilo actually wants (design-eng lead at a tier-1 client) is not hunting for a CV-generator tool; they are evaluating a practitioner who can be embedded into a team. F2 reframes Danilo as a tool-builder when his positioning is "profile + agentic AI = combination." Keep the artifact-download affordances from F2 — they should sit inside F1's hero as a secondary CTA, not replace the hero's thesis.

Final-3 is the most beautiful direction and the wrong bet for this week. The +19 hours vs F1, the PDF re-type knock-on cost, and the Fraunces load are all absorbable — but only once the cases are public, traffic exists, and Danilo has real conversion data to defend the editorial register against. Ship F1 in May, hold F3 as a v2 evolution for August once there's something to optimize.
